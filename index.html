What is the Gaokao?

For millions of Chinese students, the Gaokao, or the National College Entrance Examination, is a life-defining moment. This grueling two-day exam, held annually in June, is the sole determining factor for college admissions in China. With a history spanning over 1,300 years, the Gaokao has become a cultural institution that not only tests academic knowledge but also puts immense pressure on students and their families. The origins of the Gaokao can be traced back to the Imperial Examination System, established during the Sui Dynasty (581-618 AD). This ancient system aimed to select the most talented individuals for government positions, regardless of their social background. The modern Gaokao, however, was introduced in 1952, shortly after the founding of the People's Republic of China. It was suspended during the Cultural Revolution (1966-1976) but later reinstated in 1977.

Today, the Gaokao is a comprehensive examination that covers a wide range of subjects, including Chinese, mathematics, English, and either humanities or sciences, depending on the student's chosen track. Totaling 9 hours over three days.

But what is it like to take the Gaokao and what's its role in modern China?

For Chinese students, the Gaokao is not just an exam; it is a defining moment that requires months, if not years, of preparation. The pressure to succeed is palpable, as the test's outcome can determine a student's entire future (restrictions in colleges and subsequent career paths)

. In the weeks leading up to the Gaokao, students often intensify their study schedules, spending countless hours reviewing course material and taking practice tests . Sleep becomes a luxury, as many students prioritize every waking moment for study. The atmosphere in schools and homes is tense, with a singular focus on the impending exam.

On the day of the Gaokao, students arrive at their designated testing centers, armed with pencils, erasers, and a wealth of knowledge they hope to demonstrate. The exam itself is a marathon, spanning two days and totaling around nine hours of testing time. The first day typically covers Chinese language and mathematics, while the second day is dedicated to English, as well as either humanities or sciences, depending on the student's chosen track. The testing environment is highly regulated, with strict rules in place to prevent cheating and ensure fairness. Students are assigned to specific seats and are prohibited from bringing in any electronic devices or study aids. The pressure is high, and the stakes are even higher.

As more and more students sit for the Gaokao each year, the competition has only intensified. For many students, the Gaokao represents the culmination of their academic journey thus far. It is a test of not only their knowledge but also their endurance, dedication, and mental fortitude. The experience is intense and often emotionally charged, with students and their families investing so much in this singular moment.

In 2022, a staggering 11.93 million students took the exam, a number that has been steadily increasing over the past decade.

Beyond the exam itself, the Gaokao has a profound impact on Chinese society. The months surrounding the test see a flurry of activity, with businesses catering to the needs of students and their families. From brain-boosting snacks to good luck charms, the Gaokao has spawned an entire industry dedicated to supporting students through this challenging period. The Gaokao experience is a shared one, a rite of passage that connects generations of Chinese students.

The debate around the Gaokao

The Gaokao has been a topic of intense debate in recent years. As the country grapples with the challenges of modernization and globalization, the role of the Gaokao in the education system has come under scrutiny.

The Gaokao plays a crucial role in promoting social reform and mobility in China. Proponents argue that the standardized nature of the exam offers a level playing field for all students, regardless of their socioeconomic background. By performing well on the Gaokao, students from disadvantaged families have the opportunity to attend prestigious universities and secure high-paying jobs, thereby breaking the cycle of poverty and narrowing the socioeconomic divide in the country. Moreover, the widespread availability of resources to prepare for the Gaokao further enhances its potential as a tool for social mobility. With access to study materials, online courses, and tutoring services, students from all walks of life can adequately prepare for the exam and increase their chances of success. This accessibility ensures that a student's financial situation does not hinder their ability to excel on the Gaokao and pursue their educational and career aspirations. In addition to promoting social reform, supporters of the Gaokao emphasize its role in fostering a strong work ethic and academic rigor among Chinese students. The high-stakes nature of the exam encourages students to dedicate themselves to their studies and strive for excellence in their academic pursuits. This rigorous preparation not only improves educational outcomes but also instills valuable life skills such as discipline, perseverance, and resilience. These qualities, which are highly prized in both academic and professional settings, are seen as essential for success in an increasingly competitive global economy.

Opponents of the Gaokao argue that the exam perpetuates socioeconomic disparities rather than promoting social mobility. While the test is standardized, the resources available to prepare for it are not equally distributed. Students from wealthy families have access to better schools, private tutoring, and expensive study materials, giving them a significant advantage over their less privileged peers. This inequality is further exacerbated by the system of bonus points, which are often awarded to students from affluent areas or those with special talents. Critics argue that these bonus points disproportionately benefit students from higher socioeconomic backgrounds, undermining the meritocratic ideals of the Gaokao. Moreover, the Gaokao's impact on students' mental health is a major concern for many critics. The immense pressure to succeed on the exam, coupled with the societal obsession with academic achievement, has led to a mental health crisis among Chinese students. The stress and anxiety associated with preparing for the Gaokao have been linked to a range of psychological issues, including depression, burnout, and even suicide. Students often sacrifice their hobbies, social lives, and personal well-being in the pursuit of high scores, leading to a lack of balance and a narrowing of their overall development. The psychological toll of the Gaokao is not limited to students alone; it also affects their families. Parents often feel immense pressure to ensure their children's success. This pressure can strain family relationships and create an unhealthy and stressful home environment. Critics argue that the Gaokao's negative impact on mental health is not only detrimental to individual students and their families but also has broader societal implications, as it contributes to a culture of excessive competition and stress. Furthermore, the Gaokao's emphasis on memorization and test-taking skills has been criticized for failing to promote critical thinking, creativity, and practical skills. Opponents argue that this narrow focus on exam preparation comes at the expense of a well-rounded education that fosters innovation and prepares students for the challenges of the modern world. They contend that the Gaokao system prioritizes short-term academic success over the long-term development of a diverse and adaptable workforce, potentially hindering China's ability to compete in a rapidly changing global economy.

Where we land

Since 2012, the Ministry of Education (MOE) in China has implemented various reforms to improve the education examination system, particularly the gaokao, focusing on equality, quality, management, and oversight. These reforms have aimed to provide more equal access to higher education for both urban and rural students, especially those from central and western China and poverty-stricken areas. Between 2012 and 2022, key universities and colleges increased enrollment of students from rural or poverty-stricken areas from 10,000 to 131,000, with a total of 950,000 students enrolled during this period. Additionally, over 1.68 million migrant students have taken the gaokao in places where they study and their parents work, thanks to new policies allowing nonresident students to compete with residents on an equal footing. MOE has also revoked five conditions for bonus points given on the gaokao and plans to abolish 95 local rules that award extra points, further optimizing the preferential policy for ethnic minority students. The ministry has also developed a vocational college entrance examination system as an alternative pathway to higher education, with more than 3 million students taking the exam each year, accounting for 60% of students admitted to higher vocational education institutions. Despite the challenges posed by the Covid-19 pandemic, MOE has successfully organized the annual gaokao for over 100 million students since 2020, implementing precautionary measures and institutional changes to ensure effective organization during the normalization stage of pandemic prevention and control. Furthermore, MOE has strengthened and regulated management of the enrollment system, making information more public and cracking down on examination fraud. The comprehensive reform of the gaokao has been gradually extended to cover 29 provincial-level regions in China, with the fourth and fifth groups set to take the new gaokao in 2024 and 2025, respectively. These reforms demonstrate the Chinese government's efforts to address some of the criticisms of the gaokao system, such as socioeconomic disparities and the need for alternative pathways to higher education. By providing more equal access to education and reducing regional inequalities, the MOE aims to promote social mobility and create a more meritocratic system.

Ultimately, despite the compelling arguments on both sides of the debate surrounding the gaokao, we believe that incremental reform of the examination system is ultimately the best course of action for China. As a massive and radically diverse country, China faces unique challenges in ensuring equal access to education and promoting social mobility. While the gaokao has its flaws, it remains a powerful tool for providing opportunities to students from disadvantaged backgrounds and maintaining educational standards across the nation. Radical reform or a complete move away from standardized testing could potentially worsen the socioeconomic gaps in China. The gaokao, despite its limitations, serves as a great equalizer, allowing students from all walks of life to compete on a relatively level playing field for access to higher education and the social and economic opportunities that come with it. Incremental changes, such as the reforms implemented by the Ministry of Education since 2012, can help to address some of the system's shortcomings while preserving its core strengths.
